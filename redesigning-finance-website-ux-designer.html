Case study

Improving accessibility and navigation

Conducting a full redesign of a financial services website, led by user testing. Identifying problems, designing wireframes, creating prototypes, and iterating based on the results.

Context

Time period: July 2022 – December 2022

Role: UX/UI Designer, working in-house alongside an agency

1. Identifying the problems

Consistency

Sitewide consistency was poor, leaving the user to learn new patterns and UI elements on every page.

For example, pages were frequently dominated by a ‘multigrid’ of equal-sized tiles; when clicked, these tiles would sometimes take users to a subpage, sometimes take them to a different section of the site, sometimes open up a PDF, and sometimes do nothing at all.

We saw confusion about this time and time again on HotJar clickmaps and UserTesting videos.

Navigation

Previously there had been nobody to 'gatekeep' areas like navigation menus to make sure user experience was championed alongside competing business goals.

With no logical Information Architecture to organise content, the main navigation bar held 11 items at times: stuffed full of niche projects, short-lived events and links to external microsites.

Accessibility and inclusivity

Meanwhile, everywhere you looked, there were accessibility issues: from the standard body text size of 14px to the regular use of white/black text over images without an appropriate background overlay or outline.

Behind the scenes, images didn't have alt text, HTML heading classes were misused, and aria-labels were nonexistant.

I therefore set about collaborating with other areas of the business. I worked with my Graphic Design and Brand colleagues to improve colour contrast in their charts, and I learnt from the neurodiversity community about autoplaying videos and distracting animations.

2. Prototyping and testing at pace

The company had only just set up its UX team. So, when we arrived, the project was already partway through an unstructured discovery process that had unfortunately failed to engage with users and their problems.

Refocusing on the user

As a team, we ran week-long sprints to rapidly prototype and test design proposals based on analytics and UX best practices.

At the start of each week, I would rapidly create a handful of wireframes and we would discuss their relative merits.

Then our amazing UX Researcher set up unmoderated user tests on UserTesting to get feedback on the two most promising options. I would then assess her findings and (if necessary) make iterative tweaks to the most successful version, before presenting it back to the project group.

3. Learning from users

Discoverability

I had concerns that our core products (investment funds) were presented on incredibly dense pages.

To check this hypothesis, I indentified some key content (factsheets, ISIN numbers and portfolios) through Hotjar click maps and stakeholder input.

Then we conducted some testing, which revealed that users were indeed failing to find this content. In some cases, this was no doubt due to sidebar blindess, as crucial items were hidden in grey boxes on the side of the page.

I also analysed the scroll depth to reveal that users were giving up on scrolling down the long! page. And interviews with users suggested they were struggling to identify important items due to the poor text hierarchy...

Navigation

Navigation was clearly something we needed to solve urgently. So I created more prototypes so we could test the agency's proposed vertical tabbed navigation...

... and the possibility of horizontal tabbed navigation, which I felt would improve the discoverability of these key sections and would improve consistency between desktop and mobile.

The user testing revealed that the horizontal tabbed navigation had a higher ease of completion rating of 4.6 out 5 (compared to 4.17 out of 5). So, after a few further tweaks to the design, we were able to present our findings to the business and validate our suggestion.

4. Visual design

Collaborating

The agency's UI Designer then did a great first iteration of the visual design.

I gave input to ensure that accessibility was considered, as some 12px font sizes had crept in.

It was then my job to take all the mockups, create a Figma component library, improve consistency (in areas like interaction states), and brief the engineers...
