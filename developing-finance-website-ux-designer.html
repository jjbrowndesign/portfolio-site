Case study

Guiding development and championing users

Articulating UX design decisions for developers and negotiating with stakeholders to balance business goals with the needs of the user.

Context

Time period: February 2023 – June 2024

Role: UX/UI Designer, working in-house

1. Guiding developers

Writing annotations

After the key designs of the website redesign project had been established (get the full story here), I took the designs, recreated them using Figma components, created a design system, put the mockups back together, and then annotated them with expected interactions and behaviours.

I then worked with business analysts and devs to translate these annotations into detailed user stories, using Given-When-Then acceptance criteria, to guide development and help set restrictions for the later test and UAT phases.

Articulating design decisions

Due to the size of the project, we sometimes received push-back from product management teams at this point. It was therefore really important for me to defend key functionalities for the user, while keeping an open mind to business goals or resource issues.

A small but significant example of this was the list of Key Documents. Our user interviews had identified that financial documents like the Factsheet and Annual Report were absolutely crucial for users. But on the old site these were in 14px size font, far below the fold – or else hidden in a complex document accordion a number of clicks away. The document names were also needlessly long and repeated the name of the fund - e.g. "[Company Name] Overseas Growth Funds ICVC Annual Report - April 2023", making it hard to scan.

It seemed like an easy win to bring this further up the main page. And then we also proposed to trim the text back and call out the salient word or two that the user was searching for, e.g. "Monthly Factsheet".

But this required some backend work, and at this point in the project there was some resistance to making any backend changes, due to resource issues.

Luckily, I had some user testing videos to demonstrate how much users were struggling to find the documents. That included a handful of users saying "you had to scroll a little bit further than I'd expect to find it". But, more notably, we had one user who took a whole minute to discover it, and two users who failed to find it completely!

Backed up by this research, I was therefore able to argue the case for this change.

I'm hopeful that, when the redesigned page is up-and-running, we will be able to see a great improvement in the speed of users finding these key documents.

2. Revising designs

Receiving cross-functional feedback

Sometimes, however, it was important to adapt designs based on feedback (e.g. developers saying something would be too tricky or time-consuming to implement).

For example, on a fund page, we originally had a selector that the user could tap at the top to sort the details alphabetically by share class, distribution type or currency. This had been implemented to make things much more flexible for a user faced by some of our more complex funds, which had up to 15 options.

However, the developers said their data source provided these three pieces of information together in one string, and it would take considerably longer to break this string up and to add the sorting functionality.

Instead of forcing through the original designs, I quickly mocked up a simplified version which listed out the combined details, to see how it would look.

Making informed changes

At this stage, I remember being pressured to keep pace on the project, but I wanted to validate this design before starting development. So I assessed the UK fund matrix and concluded that three-quarters of the funds had just 1 or 2 options here. As you can imagine, this would make the original design a bit overkill.

Miller's Law tells us that users can keep around 7 items in their mind at a time, so I was happy that this wouldn't cause huge issues for funds with 7 or fewer options.

And then the remaining funds mostly differed by currency. Looking at the mockup, the content seemed quickly scannable as the shareclass item was always one letter and the second item was either 'Income' or 'Accumulation'. So I spoke to my UX Researcher and we agreed to add a task to a user test we would run when the page is live.

It only took a few hours for me to create the new mockup and think through the logic, but it saved many hours of valuable developer time – and it hopefully won't have a negative impact on the user.

It was then my job to create a design system, complete with documentation...
