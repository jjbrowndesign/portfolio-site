UX Design

Storm energy app

A native app - for both iOS and Android - that encourages users to join an eco energy provider and helps them to manage and monitor their electricity usage.

☟ Highlights of the project


1. Competitor analysis

Conducting a competitor analysis

Assessing strengths and weaknesses

Creating USPs

I quickly settled on three energy providers who would be competitors in this space: Bulb, Ovo Energy and Octopus Energy. I’m a big fan of the work that all three have done, but I still found a few weaknesses which I could address in my app. I wanted to offer:

- The ability to join on the native app, rather than being redirected to sign up via a browser.

- The ability to view full energy usage. Plus a choice between a few graph/table options and a detailed breakdown by appliances to be added in future iterations.

- Informative communications emphasising the company’s green credentials, to generate loyalty from customers.

- A friendly tone of voice and cheerful imagery.

2. User Flow Diagrams

Analysing competitors

Sketching User Flows

Finalising a User Flow Diagram

My User Flow Diagram focused on the on-boarding process, as I was keen to have registration natively on the app. Brilliant features like scanning meters using the camera (which I’ve used previously in the Bulb app) seemed crucial. And I’m keen to add a number of options at the payment screen in future, including sharing payments with partners/housemates.

3.  iOS and Android

Identifying differences between platforms

Choosing the appropriate layouts

Setting up prototypes for testing

The next step was to assess the varying opportunities offered by iOS and Android, and selecting the correct patterns to use as part of my app design. I’m currently an iPhone user, so I also borrowed a friend’s Android phone for a week to get to grips with their UI.

4. Branding

Choosing the right colour

A/B testing the logo

Finding a suitable brand

My competitor analysis made it pretty clear: if I’m creating an eco app, I need to add a shade of green. I went for quite a classic, bright green, plus a faded, pale green for secondary buttons.
The logo, slogan and wordmark were inspired by Norse mythology. I found an upbeat icon pack by Freepik and conducted some A/B testing to settle on a logo.

5.  Final product

I set up some prototypes in InVision, and iterated on the designs using feedback from fellow UI designers to reach a final product consisting of over 20 iOS and 20 Android screens.
